\begin{abstract}
This paper conducts an internal meta-analysis of the headline GDP-growth estimate in \citet{frost_madeira_mj_2025} using a structured grid of \TotalSpecs\ alternative specifications. The focal estimate - the two-way fixed-effects coefficient on freshwater withdrawal as a percentage of available freshwater - is replicated exactly ($\hat{\beta} = \BaselineBeta$, clustered s.e.\ $= \BaselineSE$, $N = \BaselineN$). Each specification is mapped into a common metric, the sample average marginal effect (AME), to ensure comparability across nonlinear and interacted models. Across the \TotalSpecs\ AMEs, the mean is \MeanAME, the median is \MedianAME, \ShareNegative\% are negative, and \ShareSigFive\% are statistically significant at the 5\% level. Quadratic and threshold forms shift the AME downward, whereas random-effects and between estimation push it toward zero. An interacted specification implies a substantially more negative scarcity--growth association in lower-income economies. The negative relationship survives the meta-analysis most clearly in within-country specifications, but its magnitude depends on functional form and on the source of identifying variation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ======================================================================

Water is a productive input with no close substitute: it irrigates crops, cools power plants, and supports industrial processes at every stage of the supply chain. Unlike most factors of production, however, water is difficult to price. Freshwater is often treated as a public good or priced far below marginal cost, creating a disconnect between observed tariffs and the resource's scarcity value \citep{oecd_2010}. The resulting overuse is compounded by the fact that water availability is unevenly distributed across space and increasingly variable over time as climate change intensifies hydrological extremes. These features make water scarcity a macroeconomically relevant constraint, yet one that standard growth models - built around capital, labor, and total factor productivity - largely ignore.

\citet{frost_madeira_mj_2025} address this gap by asking whether water scarcity has measurable consequences for GDP growth, investment, and inflation across a large cross-country panel. Their headline finding is a negative and significant association between freshwater withdrawal intensity and annual GDP growth ($\hat{\beta} = -0.102$, Table~3, column~3, p.~15). The focal metric of this study is that coefficient: it captures the estimated average change in GDP growth for a one-percentage-point increase in freshwater withdrawal as a share of available freshwater, conditional on country and year fixed effects. I replicate this estimate exactly on the 1990--2020 panel ($N = \BaselineN$, $R^2 = \BaselineRtwo$).

Measuring water scarcity at the macroeconomic level presents both opportunities and challenges. The FAO's Aquastat database provides the most comprehensive global coverage, but observations are intermittent and interpolated across years, attenuating within-country variation. National aggregates mask subnational stress, local storage capacity, and sectoral exposure. These measurement features do not invalidate the exercise - they are the best available data for a cross-country panel - but they do shape the interpretation: the estimates are associations at the country level, and they likely understate the true within-country effect because interpolation smooths the regressor relative to the dependent variable.

A single point estimate, however well-replicated, obscures the sensitivity of the result to the modeling decisions embedded in its construction. Following the internal meta-analysis approach of \citet{banzhaf_smith_2007}, I systematically vary two dimensions of modeling choice - functional form and panel estimator - crossed with three scarcity denominators, producing a grid of $3 \times 5 \times 8 = \TotalSpecs$ specifications. Each is translated into a sample average marginal effect (AME) to ensure comparability across nonlinear and interacted models. The resulting distribution is summarized by a specification curve, a histogram, and an auxiliary meta-regression that decomposes variation into contributions from each modeling decision.

Three findings emerge. First, the direction of the association is stable: \NegativeCount\ of \TotalSpecs\ AMEs are negative, and no positive estimate reaches statistical significance. Second, the magnitude is sensitive to functional form and estimator choice: the interquartile range spans [\PTwentyFiveAME, \PSeventyFiveAME], and the between estimator reverses the sign entirely. Third, the income interaction reveals that the scarcity drag is concentrated in lower-income economies, suggesting the linear baseline may understate heterogeneity in where water constraints bind most.

\section{Data}
%% ======================================================================

The analysis reuses the replication data pipeline, which merges four formatted panel sources: World Bank macroeconomic indicators, FAO Aquastat water data \citep{fao_aquastat}, Worldwide Governance Indicators, and Penn World Table series. The merged 1990--2020 panel spans \MergedCountryCount\ countries, while the regression samples cover \InternalCountryCount\ to \AvailableCountryCount\ countries depending on the scarcity denominator.

The dependent variable is annual real GDP growth in percentage points. The baseline controls are log total freshwater withdrawal per capita ($\ln W^{pc}_{ct}$) and log GDP per capita at purchasing power parity ($\ln \text{GDP}^{pc}_{ct}$). Water scarcity is measured as freshwater withdrawal divided by one of three availability denominators: internal renewable resources, total renewable resources, or available freshwater. Internal resources capture domestic hydrology only; total renewable resources add transboundary inflows; available freshwater further adjusts for environmental water requirements. Each scarcity ratio is capped at 100 percent to limit leverage from extreme observations.

Table~\ref{tab:sumstats} reports the key descriptive statistics generated directly from the extension analysis panel. The sample exhibits substantial cross-country dispersion in scarcity alongside a mean GDP growth rate close to 3\% and a standard deviation close to 6 percentage points. Two measurement issues matter for interpretation. First, Aquastat series are observed intermittently and then interpolated, smoothing within-country scarcity variation and likely attenuating fixed-effects estimates. Second, national aggregates cannot capture subnational water stress or sector-specific exposure.

\section{Model Specification}
%% ======================================================================

\subsection*{Structural motivation}

The empirical specification is motivated by a modified Cobb-Douglas production function in which water enters as an additional factor. Let $Y_{ct}$ denote output in country $c$ at time $t$, and let $K_{ct}$, $L_{ct}$, and $\text{TFP}_{ct}$ denote capital, labor, and total factor productivity. The production function takes the form
\begin{equation}
Y_{ct} = \text{TFP}_{ct} \cdot \bigl[g(W_{ct}, WS_{ct})\bigr]^{1-\alpha-\theta} \cdot K_{ct}^{\,\alpha} \cdot L_{ct}^{\,\theta},
\label{eq:production}
\end{equation}
where $W_{ct}$ is water withdrawal and $WS_{ct}$ is water scarcity. The function $g(\cdot)$ captures the effective productivity contribution of water and the penalty from scarcity, parameterized as
\begin{equation}
g(W_{ct}, WS_{ct}) = \frac{W_{ct}}{1 + WS_{ct}}.
\label{eq:g}
\end{equation}
This specification implies that effective water productivity declines as scarcity increases: $g \to W_{ct}$ as $WS_{ct} \to 0$ and $g \to 0$ as $WS_{ct} \to \infty$.

\subsection*{Log-linearization and the estimating equation}

Taking logarithms of~\eqref{eq:production} and substituting~\eqref{eq:g}:
\begin{equation}
\ln Y_{ct} = \ln \text{TFP}_{ct} + (1-\alpha-\theta)\bigl[\ln W_{ct} - \ln(1+WS_{ct})\bigr] + \alpha \ln K_{ct} + \theta \ln L_{ct}.
\label{eq:loglin}
\end{equation}
For moderate values of scarcity, a first-order Taylor expansion gives $\ln(1+WS_{ct}) \approx WS_{ct}$, so the scarcity term enters approximately linearly. This approximation deteriorates at high scarcity levels, which motivates the quadratic and threshold specifications tested in the meta-analysis.

Differencing~\eqref{eq:loglin} and replacing unobserved factor inputs with a log income proxy yields the reduced-form panel regression:
\begin{equation}
g_{ct} = \beta_1 \ln(W^{pc}_{ct}) + \beta_2 \, WS_{ct} + \beta_3 \ln(\text{GDP}^{pc}_{ct}) + \alpha_c + \alpha_t + \varepsilon_{ct},
\label{eq:panel}
\end{equation}
where $g_{ct}$ is annual GDP growth, $\alpha_c$ and $\alpha_t$ are country and year fixed effects, and standard errors are clustered by country. Two features of this transition deserve comment. First, the move from the structural model to~\eqref{eq:panel} is approximate: the log-linearization produces $\ln W_{ct}$ and $-\ln(1+WS_{ct})$, not the linear $WS_{ct}$ that enters the regression. Second, the contemporaneous $\ln(\text{GDP}^{pc}_{ct})$ control is mechanically related to GDP growth; I retain it for faithful replication, but a lagged income term would be more natural in an original growth regression.

\subsection*{Identification}

The two-way fixed-effects design absorbs time-invariant country characteristics (geography, institutions, initial conditions) and common global shocks (commodity cycles, synchronized monetary policy). After double-demeaning, the estimating equation becomes
\begin{equation}
\tilde{g}_{ct} = \beta_1 \widetilde{\ln W^{pc}_{ct}} + \beta_2 \widetilde{WS}_{ct} + \beta_3 \widetilde{\ln \text{GDP}^{pc}_{ct}} + \tilde{\varepsilon}_{ct},
\label{eq:within}
\end{equation}
where $\tilde{x}_{ct} = x_{ct} - \bar{x}_c - \bar{x}_t + \bar{x}$. The parameter $\beta_2$ identifies the scarcity--growth association under three assumptions: (i)~strict exogeneity of the regressors conditional on the fixed effects; (ii)~no time-varying confounders that correlate with both $WS_{ct}$ and $g_{ct}$ within countries; and (iii)~independence of measurement error from the true scarcity level. Assumption~(i) fails if weak growth reduces water demand, generating reverse causality. Assumption~(ii) is threatened by industrial policy changes or heterogeneous climate shocks. Assumption~(iii) is strained by the interpolation of Aquastat data, which may introduce systematic measurement error. The analysis therefore interprets $\beta_2$ as an association, not a causal effect.

\section{Analysis Design}
%% ======================================================================

To evaluate the sensitivity of the baseline estimate, I construct a 120-specification grid defined by the Cartesian product of three dimensions: 3 scarcity measures $\times$ 5 functional forms $\times$ 8 estimators. Following \citet{banzhaf_smith_2007}, the resulting estimates are collected into a specification curve and summarized by a meta-regression that identifies which modeling decisions systematically shift the scarcity coefficient.

\subsection*{Scarcity measure}

The first dimension varies the denominator of the scarcity ratio: internal renewable resources, total renewable resources, or available freshwater. These measures are highly correlated (85--95\%) but differ in scope. The authors report all three in Table~3 but do not test whether the denominator drives the result.

\subsection*{Functional form}

The second dimension varies the parametric relationship between scarcity and growth. Five specifications are tested:
\begin{align}
\text{Linear:} \quad g_{ct} &= \beta_1 WS_{ct} + X'_{ct}\delta + u_{ct}, \label{eq:linear} \\
\text{Quadratic:} \quad g_{ct} &= \beta_1 WS_{ct} + \beta_2 WS_{ct}^2 + X'_{ct}\delta + u_{ct}, \label{eq:quad} \\
\text{Income interaction:} \quad g_{ct} &= \beta_1 WS_{ct} + \beta_2 WS_{ct} \cdot \ln(\text{GDP}^{pc}_{ct}) + X'_{ct}\delta + u_{ct}, \label{eq:inc} \\
\text{Water-use interaction:} \quad g_{ct} &= \beta_1 WS_{ct} + \beta_2 WS_{ct} \cdot \ln(W^{pc}_{ct}) + X'_{ct}\delta + u_{ct}, \label{eq:use} \\
\text{Threshold:} \quad g_{ct} &= \beta_1 WS_{ct} + \beta_2 (WS_{ct} - c)_+ + X'_{ct}\delta + u_{ct}, \label{eq:thr}
\end{align}
where $(x)_+ = \max(0, x)$ and $c$ is set at the sample median. Each form is motivated by a specific economic hypothesis: the quadratic tests whether the drag from scarcity steepens at higher stress levels; the income interaction tests whether richer countries buffer scarcity through infrastructure or sectoral composition; the water-use interaction tests whether high absolute demand amplifies the scarcity penalty; and the threshold allows a discrete regime shift.

\subsection*{Estimator}

The third dimension varies the panel estimator. Two-way fixed effects (the benchmark) are compared with country FE only, random effects, the between estimator, first-differenced OLS, and \citet{machado_santos_silva_2019} panel quantile regression at the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentiles. The between estimator answers a fundamentally different question - how average growth rates covary with average scarcity \emph{across} countries - while within-country estimators identify the effect from year-to-year deviations around country means. The quantile estimator is applied without year fixed effects, following the authors' own caveat about ancillary parameters.

\subsection*{Common metric: average marginal effects}

In the linear model, the coefficient $\beta_1$ is the marginal effect at all points. In nonlinear and interacted models, the raw coefficient $\beta_1$ is evaluated at model-specific reference points (e.g., $\ln \text{GDP}^{pc} = 0$ in the income interaction) and is not directly comparable. I therefore translate every specification into its sample average marginal effect:
\begin{equation}
\text{AME}_s = \frac{1}{N_s} \sum_{(c,t) \in s} \frac{\partial \hat{g}_{ct}}{\partial WS_{ct}}.
\label{eq:ame}
\end{equation}
For the quadratic model, $\text{AME} = \hat{\beta}_1 + 2\hat{\beta}_2 \overline{WS}$. For the income interaction, $\text{AME} = \hat{\beta}_1 + \hat{\beta}_2 \overline{\ln \text{GDP}^{pc}}$. This conversion maps all specifications onto a common interpretable scale.

\subsection*{Meta-regression}

The 120 specification-level AMEs are summarized by
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{AME}_s =\ & \gamma_0 + \gamma_1 d_{\text{quad},s} + \gamma_2 d_{\text{inc},s} + \gamma_3 d_{\text{use},s} \\
& + \gamma_4 d_{\text{thr},s} + \gamma_5 d_{\text{int},s} + \gamma_6 d_{\text{ren},s} \\
& + \gamma_7 d_{\text{noyr},s} + \gamma_8 d_{\text{RE},s} + \gamma_9 d_{\text{BE},s} \\
& + \gamma_{10} d_{\text{FD},s} + \gamma_{11} d_{Q25,s} + \gamma_{12} d_{Q50,s} \\
& + \gamma_{13} d_{Q75,s} + u_s,
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:metareg}
\end{equation}
where the omitted categories are the linear functional form, the available-freshwater scarcity measure, and the two-way fixed-effects estimator. The coefficients $\gamma_j$ measure the average AME shift attributable to each modeling decision. Because all 120 specifications are estimated from the same dataset, the meta-regression coefficients are descriptive summaries of the specification space rather than formal inferential quantities, following the practice in \citet{banzhaf_smith_2007}.

\subsection*{Meta-regression}

Table~\ref{tab:meta_ame} reports the meta-regression coefficients. Four patterns stand out.

\input{../tables/meta_regression_ame.tex}

\textit{Functional form matters.} Relative to the linear baseline, both the quadratic (\QuadShift) and threshold (\ThresholdShift) specifications shift the AME downward, indicating that allowing for nonlinearity amplifies the estimated scarcity drag. The income interaction also shifts the average AME downward by \IncomeShift, whereas the water-use interaction has almost no effect.

\textit{Estimator choice reveals a within--between divergence.} The between estimator shifts the AME upward by \BetweenShift\ and random effects by \RandomEffectsShift. Added to the baseline constant reported in Table~\ref{tab:meta_ame}, the between estimator yields a mildly positive average effect - a sign reversal indicating that long-run cross-sectional differences between water-scarce and water-abundant countries look very different from the within-country relationship. This divergence supports the authors' use of fixed-effects estimation.

\textit{Year effects matter.} Dropping year fixed effects shifts the AME downward by \NoYearShift, confirming that global time trends partially offset the within-country scarcity signal in the two-way FE baseline.

\textit{Quantile patterns are suggestive.} Q25 shifts the AME by \QTwentyFiveShift, Q50 by \QFiftyShift, and Q75 by \QSeventyFiveShift, consistent with the interpretation that scarcity drag is strongest in low-growth episodes. These estimates are appropriately caveated because the quantile estimator omits year fixed effects.

\subsection*{Income heterogeneity}

The income interaction deserves separate attention because it reveals economically important heterogeneity. In the available-freshwater two-way FE interaction model, the fitted marginal effect is \MEAtLnEight\ at $\ln \text{GDP}^{pc} = 8$, \MEAtLnTen\ at $\ln \text{GDP}^{pc} = 10$, and \MEAtLnEleven\ at $\ln \text{GDP}^{pc} = 11$. The interaction curve reaches the linear benchmark around $\ln \text{GDP}^{pc} = \IncomeBenchmarkCross$, while the estimation-sample mean and median are \IncomeSampleMean\ and \IncomeSampleMedian. Figure~\ref{fig:income} visualizes this gradient.

\subsection*{Threshold diagnostic}

The full grid of \TotalSpecs\ specifications uses a median split for the threshold model. A supplementary grid search over cutoffs of 10, 20, 30, 40, and 50 percent for the available-freshwater two-way FE model finds the residual sum of squares minimized at a \BestThreshold\% cutoff, with RSS \BestThresholdRSS\ and baseline scarcity slope \BestThresholdSlope\ (Table~\ref{tab:threshold_grid}). This suggests that if a regime change exists, it likely appears at substantially higher scarcity levels than the median split implies.

\section{Conclusions}
%% ======================================================================

This paper evaluated the stability of a single result from \citet{frost_madeira_mj_2025}: the available-freshwater two-way fixed-effects coefficient in the GDP-growth regression. That estimate is reproduced exactly, and the internal meta-analysis shows that its negative sign survives a broad set of alternative modeling choices, most clearly in within-country specifications.

The substantive message is not that every model estimates the same parameter and happens to agree. Different econometric choices answer different questions. Within-country estimators deliver negative scarcity--growth associations of economically meaningful magnitude, whereas cross-sectional estimators move the effect toward zero or mildly positive values, indicating that long-run country differences are not a valid proxy for the within-country relationship.

The AME framework makes these comparisons interpretable. Once nonlinear and interacted models are mapped into averaged marginal effects, the income interaction becomes a transparent heterogeneity result: poorer economies face a substantially larger scarcity drag than richer ones. The threshold result is disciplined by the diagnostic showing the median split is not the RSS-minimizing cutoff. And the quantile results remain suggestive but appropriately caveated given the omission of year effects.

Several limitations warrant attention. The identifying strategy cannot rule out reverse causality or time-varying omitted variables; the estimates are associations, not causal effects. The meta-regression treats 120 estimates from the same dataset as observations, so its standard errors are descriptive rather than inferential. And the Aquastat interpolation likely attenuates within-country estimates toward zero.

Extensions that would strengthen the analysis include expanding the grid along sample composition (OECD vs.\ non-OECD, excluding 2020), testing for cross-sectional dependence with Driscoll-Kraay standard errors, and pursuing instrumental-variables strategies based on exogenous rainfall variation to move beyond the associational framework.
